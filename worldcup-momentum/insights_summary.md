Spain have never trailed in this tournament. Not once across seven matches. Three knockout matches followed the 2–1 script — score first, absorb an equalizer, score again to win (Austria R32, Portugal R16, Belgium QF). The France semifinal extended that dominance one step further: a 2–0 win via Oyarzabal's penalty at 22' and Porro at 58', with France scoring zero. Spain never needed to respond to an equalizer in that match. Their score differential has never crossed zero in the negative direction in any match this tournament. Their momentum line has never dipped below the baseline. Zero extra-time minutes played. It is the most statistically controlled campaign in this dataset.

Argentina have been behind in two of their four knockout matches — 0–2 against Egypt, 0–1 against England — and won both. They spent 68 minutes trailing against Egypt and 30 minutes against England: 98 combined minutes at a deficit that they converted into wins. The decisive goal in each comeback was delivered at 90+2' by a player other than Messi: Enzo Fernández against Egypt, Lautaro Martínez against England. Messi's contribution to the two recoveries was the 83rd-minute equalizer against Egypt. The squad demonstrated that it can close out pressure situations without depending on its best player to score the winner.

The extra-time load entering the final is meaningful but smaller than the original reconstruction suggested. Argentina played extra time in two matches — R32 vs Cape Verde (3–1 AET) and QF vs Switzerland (3–1 AET) — roughly 60 additional minutes beyond 90. The SF vs England ended in regulation stoppage time, Lautaro's 90+2' winner coming without the need for an extra 30 minutes. Four of Argentina's 19 goals (21%) came in genuine extra time; the two further 90+2' winners against Egypt and England are stoppage-time goals in matches that never went to extra time, and count as such.

Argentina's quarterfinal against Switzerland was not a comeback. Argentina scored first through Messi at the 10th minute, Switzerland equalized through Embolo at 67', and Argentina won in extra time. That is the same structural shape as every Spain knockout match — led, equalized, won — except Argentina needed extra time to close it. The Switzerland match produced zero sign-flips in the score differential, matching Spain's 0 across all seven matches. Argentina's real comeback count is two, not three.

Both teams responded to every concession this tournament — eight concession events, zero matches where the team that was scored against failed to score back before the final whistle. (Spain never conceded in the France semifinal, so that match has no entry.) Response times range from 5 minutes (Mac Allister at 92' in ET vs Cape Verde, conceded at 87') to 47 minutes (Merino at 88' vs Belgium, where De Ketelaere had equalized at 41'). Spain's outlier is Belgium: they endured 47 minutes level before Merino won it, significantly longer than their other two knockout responses (7 min vs Portugal, 17 min vs Austria). Argentina's longest response was 45 minutes (Mac Allister equalizing Embolo at 67' with a goal at 112' in Switzerland ET). The 30-minute gap vs England — Kane at 55', Enzo at 85' — is the longest gap in a match Argentina actually needed to come back from a deficit.

The pre-comeback momentum analysis reveals a structural limitation of the proxy. For Argentina's two comeback matches, the momentum value at 10 minutes before the equalizing goal was not at the match's lowest point — in one case it was not even negative. Against Egypt, the momentum nadir was −1.33 at minute 34 (right after Elneny's second Egyptian goal). By the time Messi equalized at 83', momentum was already +0.23 — positive — because the Egyptian goals from 15' and 34' had decayed almost entirely and Di Maria's 56' goal had taken over. The model had forgotten the deficit before the comeback arrived. Against England, the nadir was −1.00 at minute 55 (right after Kane's goal). At 75' — ten minutes before Enzo's equalizer — momentum had recovered to −0.32, only 31% of the trough value. The "deep dip right before the comeback" pattern holds for neither match.

This is not a flaw to fix but a property to report. The 12-minute half-life means the model holds adversity in memory for roughly 30–40 minutes before decaying it to near zero. Long comeback matches — where a deficit set up at minute 15 or 34 persists until minute 83 or 85 — will show recovered or positive momentum by the time the response arrives, simply because the concessions are old. What the proxy captures accurately is the immediate aftermath of a goal (impulse and decay). What it cannot capture is the accumulated pressure of playing behind for an hour, the tactical adjustments, or the psychological weight of a 0–2 scoreboard. The broadcast "Match Momentum" graphic implies those things. This proxy, honestly labeled, does not.

The Argentina–England explainer chart shows the tension the proxy holds and conceals at once. The chart is genuinely useful for showing recent pressure in real time: a spike or drop tells you something just happened and is still resonating — the line correctly fires at Gordon's goal at 55', immediately registering the adversity. It is genuinely misleading if a viewer reads a high line as a dominant team rather than a recently active one. By minute 72, well before Enzo's equalizer, the line had already recovered nearly half its deficit — not because Argentina had turned the match around, but because the 12-minute half-life had forgotten most of the Gordon impulse. And from minutes 0 to 54, during a tense goalless opening that told its own story on the pitch, the proxy showed nothing at all. Both things are true at once: immediate responsiveness at the exact moment of a goal, structural silence everywhere else. That tension — what the chart lights up versus what it cannot see — is the actual finding about this model, not a caveat around it.

Spain's goal distribution peaks sharply in the 31st-to-45th-minute window (31% of goals) and has a hard floor at 90 minutes — nothing in stoppage time, nothing in extra time. Argentina's distribution is the mirror image: 57.9% of goals arrive from the 76th minute onward, including 21.1% in genuine extra time. Spain's Belgium quarterfinal winner came from Merino in the 88th minute, the latest Spain have scored all tournament. Argentina's last four knockout goals all arrived at 85' or later.

The momentum proxy built here is not a forecast. It is a decay curve over goal events with a documented smoothing parameter and a stated formula — which is more than the Opta graphic offers, but is still not a match predictor. The validation pass found 31.0% directional accuracy across 42 goal events, below chance, confirming that goal-timing alone does not tell you who scores next. The patterns above are descriptive.

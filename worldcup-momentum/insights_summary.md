Spain have never trailed in this tournament. Not once across seven matches. Every knockout game followed the same pattern: score first, absorb an equalizer, score again to win 2-1. Their score differential has never crossed zero in the negative direction, their momentum line has never dipped below the baseline. Zero extra-time minutes played. It is the most statistically controlled campaign in this dataset.

Argentina have been behind in three of their four knockout matches — 0-2 against Egypt, 0-1 against Switzerland, 0-1 against England — and won all three. Six of their nineteen goals (32%) came in extra time, which they have played three times to Spain's zero. Messi has scored eight goals, but Argentina's last two knockout wins came without him scoring in regulation; the squad has demonstrated it can win without leaning on him.

The extra-time load is the most concrete asymmetry entering the final: Argentina have played roughly ninety minutes of football beyond the ninetieth minute that Spain have not, and it shows up in every late-goal metric in the dataset.

Spain's goal distribution peaks in the 31st-to-45th-minute window (31% of goals) and has a hard floor at minute 90 — nothing in stoppage time, nothing in extra time. Argentina's distribution does the opposite: flat through 75 minutes, then 52% of goals arriving from the 76th minute onward, with a pronounced extra-time cluster. The two profiles do not just look different; they describe teams that finish matches in structurally opposite ways.

Messi's eight goals represent 42% of Argentina's total, which is not meaningfully different from Oyarzabal's 38% share for Spain. Both teams have concentration in one player and some distribution in the rest. The more relevant data point is that Argentina went through to the final without Messi scoring in regulation in either the quarterfinal or the semifinal, which suggests the machine does not require him to produce on any given night.

The momentum proxy built here is not a forecast. It is a decay curve over goal events with a documented smoothing parameter, which is more than the Opta graphic offers, but it is still not a match predictor — the validation pass found 36.6% directional accuracy, below chance, confirming that goal-timing alone does not predict who scores next. The patterns above are descriptive. The match will be decided by football.
